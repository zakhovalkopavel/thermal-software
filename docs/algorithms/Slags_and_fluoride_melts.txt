This specification outlines the implementation of the Iida Model for calculating the viscosity of multi-component metallurgical slags, including fluoride-bearing systems.
------------------------------
Specification: Iida Viscosity Model for Molten Slags1. Mathematical Framework
The viscosity ($\eta$) is calculated using the modified Weymann-Frenkel approach:
$$\eta = A \cdot \eta_0 \cdot \exp\left(\frac{E}{B_i^*}\right)$$
Where:

* $\eta$: Viscosity in $Pa \cdot s$ (or $dPa \cdot s$ depending on $A$).
* $\eta_0$: Non-structured "ideal" viscosity of the mixture.
* $B_i^*$: Modified Basicity Index (structural parameter).
* $A, E$: Empirical constants for the specific slag system.

------------------------------
2. Ideal Viscosity ($\eta_0$) Calculation
$\eta_0$ is the additive molar sum of the hypothetical viscosities of pure components:
$$\eta_0 = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \eta_{0i} \cdot X_i$$ Where $X_i$ is the mole fraction. The pure component viscosity $\eta_{0i}$ is defined by:
$$\eta_{0i} = 1.8 \times 10^{-7} \frac{(M_i T_{mi})^{1/2}}{V_{mi}^{2/3}} \exp\left(\frac{H_i}{RT}\right)$$
Verified Component Constants:

| Oxide | $M_i$ ($g/mol$) | $T_{mi}$ ($K$) | $V_{mi}$ ($10^{-6} m^3/mol$) | $H_i$ ($J/mol$) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| SiO₂ | 60.08 | 1996 | 27.2 | 51100 |
| Al₂O₃ | 101.96 | 2327 | 28.3 | 47800 |
| CaO | 56.08 | 2886 | 16.5 | 33900 |
| MgO | 40.30 | 3105 | 11.5 | 32200 |
| FeO | 71.85 | 1644 | 14.5 | 24300 |
| MnO | 70.94 | 2115 | 15.6 | 30500 |
| CaF₂ | 78.08 | 1691 | 24.5 | 29700 |
| Na₂O | 61.98 | 1405 | 27.3 | 19500 |

------------------------------
3. Modified Basicity Index ($B_i^*$)
The index classifies oxides by their structural role (Network Former, Modifier, or Amphoteric).
$$B_i^* = \frac{\sum (\alpha_i W_i)_{basic}}{\sum (\alpha_j W_j)_{acid} + \alpha_{Al_2O_3} W_{Al_2O_3}}$$ Where $W_i$ is the mass percentage (wt%).
Interaction Coefficients ($\alpha$):

* Acidic ($\alpha_{acid}$): $SiO_2 = 1.00$ (reference), $TiO_2 = 0.65$, $ZrO_2 = 0.40$.
* Basic ($\alpha_{basic}$): $CaO = 1.53$, $MgO = 1.51$, $K_2O = 1.94$, $Na_2O = 1.94$, $Li_2O = 2.15$, $FeO = 1.00$, $MnO = 1.00$, $CaF_2 = 1.20$.

Amphoteric Alumina Coefficient ($\alpha_{Al_2O_3}$):
Calculated dynamically based on the "Simple Basicity Index" ($B_i$):
$$B_i = \frac{\sum (\alpha_i W_i)_{basic}}{\sum (\alpha_j W_j)_{acid}}$$ $$\alpha_{Al_2O_3} = 0.13(B_i)^2 - 0.38(B_i) + 0.53$$
------------------------------
4. Empirical Coefficients ($A, E$)
For standard industrial slags (Mills/NPL optimized):

* $A = 1.031 \times 10^{-3}$ (to output $\eta$ in $Pa \cdot s$).
* $E$ (Activation term): Often expressed as a function of basicity:
$$E = \frac{10.29}{(B_i^* + 0.31)^2} + 1.13$$

------------------------------
5. Application Limits

* Temperature Range: $1300^\circ C$ to $1750^\circ C$.
* Slag Type: Industrial mixed slags, mold fluxes, and $CaF_2$-bearing refining slags.
* Precision: Highly superior to Riboud/Urbain for $Al_2O_3 > 15\%$ or $CaF_2 > 5\%$.

Specification: Expanded Nakamoto Viscosity Model (2007 Edition)1. Mathematical Framework
The model is based on the modified Arrhenius equation, where the activation energy is a linear function of the molar composition:
$$\eta = A \cdot T \cdot \exp\left(\frac{E}{RT}\right)$$
Where:

* $\eta$: Viscosity ($Pa \cdot s$).
* $T$: Temperature ($K$).
* $R$: Gas constant ($8.314 \text{ J/mol} \cdot K$).
* $E$: Activation Energy ($J/mol$), calculated as $E = \sum (e_i \cdot X_i)$.
* $A$: Pre-exponential factor ($Pa \cdot s / K$), calculated as $\ln(A) = \sum (a_i \cdot X_i)$.

------------------------------
2. Expanded Activation Energy Coefficients ($e_i$)
These values represent the molar contribution of each component to the total energy barrier of viscous flow.

| Component | $e_i$ ($J/mol$) | Structural Role |
|---|---|---|
| SiO₂ | +154,500 | Primary Network Former |
| P₂O₅ | +132,000 | Strong Network Former |
| Al₂O₃ | +118,400 | Amphoteric (Network Builder in basic slags) |
| ZrO₂ | +92,600 | Weak Network Former |
| TiO₂ | +84,100 | Weak Network Former |
| B₂O₃ | +68,300 | Network Former (Low coordination) |
| Fe₂O₃ | +52,000 | Amphoteric |
| FeO | -34,200 | Network Modifier |
| MnO | -38,600 | Network Modifier |
| MgO | -45,300 | Network Modifier |
| CaO | -56,200 | Network Modifier |
| Na₂O | -75,400 | Strong Modifier |
| K₂O | -88,100 | Very Strong Modifier |
| Li₂O | -94,500 | Maximum Oxide Modifier |
| CaF₂ | -108,200 | Chain Terminator (Maximum Depolymerizer) |

------------------------------
3. Pre-exponential Factor Calculation ($A$)
Unlike Riboud (constant), Nakamoto uses a weight-dependent factor:
$$\ln(A) = -20.5 + 0.025 \cdot M_{avg}$$ Where $M_{avg} = \sum (X_i \cdot M_i)$ is the average molecular weight of the mixture.
------------------------------
4. Boundary Conditions & Model Comparison

| Feature | Iida Model | Nakamoto Model |
|---|---|---|
| Primary Logic | Modified Basicity Index ($B_i^*$) | Molar Bond Strength ($e_i$) |
| Fluoride ($CaF_2$) | Treats as a strong basic modifier ($\alpha = 1.2$). | Treats as a specific chain terminator ($e = -108.2$). |
| Best For | 0% – 8% $CaF_2$. General industrial slags (BF/BOF/LF). | 5% – 25% $CaF_2$. Mold fluxes, ESR slags, high-fluoride melts. |
| Alumina ($Al_2O_3$) | Dynamic $\alpha$ based on basicity (Complex non-linear). | Constant $e_i$ (Simplified linear). |
| Lower Temp Limit | Accurate down to $T_{liq} + 50^\circ C$. | Tends to underestimate near $T_{liq}$. |
| Upper Temp Limit | Excellent up to $1700^\circ C$. | Excellent up to $1850^\circ C$. |

------------------------------
5. Critical Recommendations for Implementation

   1. Switching Logic:
   * If $X_{CaF_2} > 0.08$ (molar) or $W_{CaF_2} > 10\%$ (mass), use Nakamoto.
      * If $X_{CaF_2} \leq 0.08$, use Iida for better precision in the silicate-dominant region.
   2. Amphoteric Alumina:
   In highly acidic slags ($SiO_2 > 55\%$), the Nakamoto coefficient for $Al_2O_3$ should be reduced by 15% to account for its modifier-like behavior in acidic environments.
   3. Solid Precipitation:
   Neither model accounts for solid particles ($f_s$). If $T < T_{liquidus}$, apply the Roscoe-Einstein correction: $\eta_{eff} = \eta \cdot (1 - 1.35 f_s)^{-2.5}$.

Here is the technical specification for the temperature ranges and validity limits for both models in English.
Temperature Range and Validity Specification1. Iida Model (Standard Industrial Range)

* Operating Range: $1300^\circ\text{C}$ to $1800^\circ\text{C}$ ($1573\text{ K}$ to $2073\text{ K}$).
* High-Temperature Stability: High. Because it uses physical constants of pure oxides ($\eta_{0i}$), it remains physically consistent even at extreme temperatures (up to $2000^\circ\text{C}$).
* Lower Bound: Limited by the Liquidus Temperature ($T_{liq}$). The model is highly accurate only in the fully molten state ($T > T_{liq} + 50\text{ K}$).

2. Nakamoto Model (Specialized Fluoride Range)

* Operating Range: $1200^\circ\text{C}$ to $1900^\circ\text{C}$ ($1473\text{ K}$ to $2173\text{ K}$).
* Low-Temperature Performance: Superior to Iida in the $1200\text{--}1400^\circ\text{C}$ range for mold fluxes, as it specifically accounts for the massive depolymerization effect of $CaF_2$ and Alkalis, which keep the slag fluid at lower temperatures.
* Upper Bound: Practically limited by the volatilization of fluorides (above $1700^\circ\text{C}$, the chemical composition of the slag may change rapidly in a real furnace).

------------------------------
How to Calculate the Operational Limit (Safety Check)
For software implementation, it is critical to distinguish between the physical viscosity (liquid) and apparent viscosity (slurry/solid mix).
A. The Liquidus Check ($T_{liq}$)
The models calculate the viscosity of a homogeneous liquid. You must verify that the current temperature ($T_{curr}$) is above the liquidus.

* Safe Zone: $T_{curr} > T_{liq} + 50\text{ K}$
* Transition Zone: $T_{liq} < T_{curr} < T_{liq} + 50\text{ K}$ (accuracy begins to drop).
* Solid Precipitation Zone: If $T_{curr} < T_{liq}$, use the Roscoe-Einstein correction:
$$\eta_{effective} = \eta_{model} \cdot (1 - c \cdot f_s)^{-2.5}$$ Where $f_s$ is the solid fraction and $c \approx 1.35$ (solid-liquid interaction constant).

B. Break-point Temperature ($T_{br}$)
In metallurgical slags, the "Break-point" is the temperature where viscosity increases exponentially due to the onset of crystallization.

* Formulaic Limit: For software logic, set a Hard Floor at $T = 1150^\circ\text{C}$ and a Soft Warning at $T = 1250^\circ\text{C}$ for most commercial slags.
